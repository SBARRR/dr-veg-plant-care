## Watering

- How to check: Check the top 1–2 inches of mix, pot weight, and drainage behavior. Water when the upper mix is dry and the pot feels lighter, not on a fixed calendar.
  <!-- How the user should judge whether watering is needed. Include practical methods like pot weight, finger check, tray level, leaf firmness, caudex feel, skewer test, or visual cues. -->

- Water type: Regular tap water is generally acceptable unless local water is extremely softened or salty.

- Watering method: Top-water thoroughly until water drains out, then let excess drain away. Do not leave the pot sitting in standing water.

## Maintenance

- Pests: Watch for scale, especially cycad aulacaspis scale, plus mealybugs and spider mites. Check undersides of fronds, the crown, and stem. For scale, horticultural oil can help, but coverage must hit undersides and stems; repeat treatment may be needed. Do not spray oils in extreme heat or direct hot sun.

- Repotting: Repot only when needed: poor drainage, root crowding, unstable pot, compacted media, or decline linked to media problems. Best done during warm active growth. Avoid unnecessary disturbance because sagos are slow and can sulk after root disruption.

- Soil changes: Refresh or replace media only when drainage has degraded, salts have built up, or the mix is staying too wet. Partial refresh is safer than aggressive full bare-rooting unless rot or severe media failure is suspected.

- Root pruning: Tolerates only cautious, limited root work. Avoid heavy root pruning unless absolutely necessary for bonsai/container management, and do it during warm active growth with excellent aftercare.

- Pruning: Remove only fully dead, brown fronds or badly pest-infested fronds. Do not remove green or mostly green fronds just for appearance; they still feed the plant.

## Fertilizer

- Fertilizer preference: Slow-release palm/cycad-style fertilizer with magnesium and micronutrients is preferred. Avoid high-nitrogen lawn fertilizer.
  <!-- General fertilizer profile this plant type prefers, including nutrient balance, strength sensitivity, and anything to avoid. -->

- Fertilizer form: Best: slow-release granular palm fertilizer. Acceptable: mild balanced slow-release pellets at conservative dose. Least ideal: frequent liquid fertilizer, because it is easier to overdo and can leach quickly.

- When to fertilize: During active warm-season growth only, mainly spring through summer.

## Seasonal Adjustments

- Spring / summer: Expect most growth during warm weather. Water more often as heat, sun, and airflow increase. Fertilize lightly only if the plant is stable and actively growing.
  <!-- Guidance for warm-season or active-growth care changes, such as watering frequency, light exposure, fertilizing, outdoor placement, heat management, or growth expectations. -->

- Fall / winter: Reduce watering frequency as growth slows and evaporation drops. Skip fertilizer. Protect from frost and freezing temperatures.

- Dormancy notes: Not true dormancy like a bulb, but growth can slow heavily in cool weather. Older fronds may age gradually; sudden widespread yellowing or collapse is not normal.

## Common Problems
<!-- Common non-emergency issues this plant type tends to have, including likely causes and early warning patterns. Use this for problems worth watching but not immediate crisis signs. -->

- Yellowing older fronds: Can be normal aging, nutrient deficiency, overwatering, underwatering, or root stress. Do not remove until mostly brown unless pests are involved.

- Brown crispy tips or patches: Often sun scorch, drought stress, salt buildup, fertilizer burn, or old damage.

- Slow/no growth: Often normal; sago palms are naturally slow. Lack of new flushes is not automatically a problem if existing fronds are firm and green.

- Scale insects: White crusty specks or patches, especially on undersides of fronds and stems. Heavy infestations can cause yellowing, browning, and decline.

- Root rot risk: Wet, stagnant, poorly drained media can rot roots and crown tissues.

## Danger Signs
<!-- Serious symptoms that may require urgent action or close inspection. Include signs of rot, severe dehydration, pest infestation, collapse, spreading black/brown tissue, or other plant-specific emergencies. -->

- Crown feels soft, mushy, or smells rotten.

- New central growth collapses, blackens, or pulls out easily.

- Trunk/caudex becomes soft or sunken.

- Widespread fast yellowing or browning across many fronds.

- Heavy white scale crust on fronds, crown, stem, or roots.

- Pot stays wet for many days after watering.

- Pet or child chews any part of the plant, especially seeds.

## Rescue / Recovery
<!-- Guidance for stabilizing a stressed, neglected, newly purchased, damaged, or declining plant of this type. Focus on what to do first, what to avoid, and how quickly to intervene. -->

- First stabilize: give bright filtered light, good airflow, and hold fertilizer.

- Check moisture and drainage before watering again.

- If overwatered or rot is suspected, inspect the crown/trunk firmness and consider checking roots/media before adding more water.

- If pest pressure is visible, identify the pest before treating. For scale, target undersides of fronds and stems; old dead scale may remain stuck even after treatment works.

- Avoid stacking stress: do not repot, prune heavily, fertilize, and spray all at once unless the plant is in immediate danger.

## Hard No's
<!-- Things Dr. Veg should generally avoid recommending for this plant type because they are risky, harmful, or commonly misunderstood. -->

- Do not keep it sitting in standing water.

- Do not fertilize a stressed, sick, recently repotted, or cold-damaged sago.

- Do not remove green fronds just to make it look cleaner.

- Do not use high-nitrogen lawn fertilizer.

- Do not spray horticultural oil in hot direct sun or extreme heat.

- Do not assume it is pet-safe; all parts are toxic, especially seeds.

- Do not aggressively bare-root or heavy-root-prune unless there is a clear reason.

## Miscellaneous Notes
<!-- Useful care information that does not fit cleanly into the sections above. Keep this species/type-level, not specific to the user's individual plant. -->

- Sago palm is not a true palm; it is a cycad.

- It is very slow-growing, so “no visible change” can be normal.

- Works well as a container or bonsai-style plant, but shallow pots dry faster and leave less buffer against heat, fertilizer mistakes, and watering mistakes.

- New fronds usually emerge in flushes rather than steady continuous growth.
