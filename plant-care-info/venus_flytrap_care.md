- How to check: Check tray level and media moisture. The media should feel wet to damp at all times, not dry or crunchy.

- Water type: Distilled, rain, or reverse-osmosis water only unless tap water is confirmed very low TDS, ideally under about 50–90 ppm.

- Watering method: Bottom-water by keeping a shallow amount of pure water in the tray. Let the tray get low or briefly empty before refilling if conditions are cool/cloudy, but do not let the media dry out.

## Maintenance

## Seasonal Adjustments

- Spring / summer: Main active growth season. Keep tray water available, give as much safe bright light as possible, and let it catch insects naturally outdoors.
  <!-- Guidance for warm-season or active-growth care changes, such as watering frequency, light exposure, fertilizing, outdoor placement, heat management, or growth expectations. -->

- Fall / winter: Expect slower growth and some trap dieback. Keep media damp, use less tray depth in cool/cloudy weather, and protect from hard freezes.

- Dormancy notes: Venus flytraps are temperate carnivorous plants and may slow down in winter. Do not force refrigerator dormancy for this plant unless there is a specific reason and a careful plan. In mild Florida porch conditions, manage by light, moisture, and temperature rather than panic.

## Common Problems
<!-- Common non-emergency issues this plant type tends to have, including likely causes and early warning patterns. Use this for problems worth watching but not immediate crisis signs. -->

- Black traps: Individual traps naturally die after age, digestion, or repeated closing. This is normal if the crown is healthy and new growth continues.

- Weak floppy growth: Usually low light.

- Brown crispy edges: Can come from drying out, mineral buildup, heat stress, or sudden light change.

- Mold on dead traps/media: Often from stagnant wet conditions plus dead material. Remove fully dead tissue and improve airflow.

- Fungus gnats: Common in wet media. Usually not urgent unless larvae are damaging weak plants.

- No red inside traps: Often not enough light, though some varieties stay greener.

- Slow recovery after store purchase: Common. Big-box flytraps are often stressed from poor light, bad water, or packaging.

## Danger Signs
<!-- Serious symptoms that may require urgent action or close inspection. Include signs of severe dehydration, pest infestation, collapse, spreading black or brown tissue, or other plant-specific emergencies. -->

- Media dries out fully.

- Plant received tap, bottled, softened, or mineral-heavy water repeatedly.

- Crown/rhizome turns mushy, black, or foul-smelling.

- All new growth emerges black or collapses.

- No firm green growth remains.

- Severe aphid distortion on new traps.

- Pot/tray becomes hot enough to cook the roots in direct sun.

## Rescue / Recovery
<!-- Guidance for stabilizing a stressed, neglected, newly purchased, damaged, or declining plant of this type. Focus on what to do first, what to avoid, and how quickly to intervene. -->

- Stabilize first: bright filtered light, pure water, wet media, and good airflow.

- Do not fertilize, repot, or feed heavily while stressed unless the media/water situation is actively harmful.

- If the plant came from a store cube or poor conditions, acclimate to stronger light gradually over several days.

- Remove only fully black dead traps; leave green tissue alone.

- If wrong water was used once or twice, flush from the top with distilled/rain/RO water and resume tray watering.

- If media smells rotten or appears unsuitable, repot into carnivorous-safe media.

- Let it catch bugs naturally outdoors; do not rush feeding a weak plant.

## Hard No's
<!-- Things Dr. Veg should generally avoid recommending for this plant type because they are risky, harmful, or commonly misunderstood. -->

- Do not use regular potting soil.

- Do not use fertilizer, compost, or nutrient-rich amendments.

- Do not use tap water unless confirmed very low-TDS.

- Do not feed meat, hamburger, lunch meat, fruit, candy, or human food.

- Do not trigger traps for fun.

- Do not let the media dry out.

- Do not keep it in a sealed terrarium with stagnant air.

- Do not assume black individual traps mean the whole plant is dying.

## Miscellaneous Notes
<!-- Useful care information that does not fit cleanly into the sections above. Keep this species/type-level, not specific to the user's individual plant. -->

- Venus flytrap is native to coastal North Carolina and a small area of South Carolina, not a tropical jungle plant.

- It is a bog plant: wet roots, poor acidic media, strong light, and pure water are the core care pillars.

- Outdoor plants usually catch enough insects on their own.

- Store-bought plants often need stabilization more than intervention.

- The crown/rhizome health matters more than any single ugly trap.
