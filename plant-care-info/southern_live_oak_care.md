## Watering

- How to check: Check the top 2–3 inches of soil, pot weight, drainage speed, and leaf posture. For a recently transplanted or root-damaged tree, aim for steady moisture without sogginess.
  <!-- How the user should judge whether watering is needed. Include practical methods like pot weight, finger check, tray level, leaf firmness, caudex feel, skewer test, or visual cues. -->

- Water type: Tap water or rainwater is generally acceptable. Avoid softened/salty water if possible.
  <!-- Type of water this plant can safely receive. Example: tap water acceptable, distilled/rain/RO only, avoid softened water, etc. -->

- Watering method: Top-water deeply until water exits the drainage holes, then let the pot drain fully. Do not leave the pot standing in water.

- Common watering mistakes: Watering on a calendar, giving shallow sips, assuming rain reached the pot on a screened porch, keeping the soil constantly wet, or letting a recovering potted oak dry hard.

## Maintenance

- Pests: Usually fairly problem-free, but watch for galls, scale, aphids, mites, caterpillars, leaf miners, and borers. Galls are often cosmetic. Borers are more concerning because they tend to attack stressed trees. Powdery mildew and leaf spots can occur, especially with low airflow.

- Repotting: Repot only when needed: poor drainage, root crowding, unstable container, or planned bonsai development. For bonsai/container work, late winter to early spring before a strong growth flush is usually safest. Avoid repotting while the tree is recovering from transplant shock.

- Soil changes: Shift gradually toward a coarse, well-draining container/bonsai mix over time. Avoid unnecessary full soil changes unless drainage has failed or roots need inspection.

- Root pruning: Live oak can be trained for bonsai, but root reduction should be gradual. Do not remove more than about one-third of the roots in a single session, and avoid root pruning entirely while stressed.

- Pruning: Structural pruning is important while young. Remove dead, damaged, crossing, or poorly placed branches gradually. For bonsai development, hard pruning is best reserved for a vigorous tree before active spring growth. Avoid full defoliation.

- Pot size: For development, use a roomy training pot or grow box to build strength and trunk size. A shallow bonsai pot should come later, after the tree is vigorous and has a healthier, denser root system.

## Seasonal Adjustments

- Spring / summer: Main growth season. Increase watering checks as heat rises. Fertilize only if the tree is stable and actively growing. Major pruning or bonsai work should only happen if the tree is vigorous.
  <!-- Guidance for warm-season or active-growth care changes, such as watering frequency, light exposure, fertilizing, outdoor placement, heat management, or growth expectations. -->

- Fall / winter: Reduce watering frequency as growth slows. Skip fertilizer. Protect container roots from unusual cold snaps.

- Dormancy notes: Southern live oak is semi-evergreen to evergreen, not a classic leafless winter dormant tree. It may shed older leaves before a spring flush; that can be normal if new growth follows.

## Common Problems
<!-- Common non-emergency issues this plant type tends to have, including likely causes and early warning patterns. Use this for problems worth watching but not immediate crisis signs. -->

- Transplant shock: Wilting, leaf drop, slow growth, or crispy edges after digging/moving. Stabilize water and light; avoid pruning/fertilizing unless necessary.

- Leaf scorch: Often from sudden sun increase, hot dry wind, drought stress, root damage, or salt/fertilizer burn.

- Root rot: Risk rises when container soil stays wet, heavy, and poorly aerated.

- Galls: Common on oaks and usually cosmetic rather than dangerous.

- Powdery mildew or leaf spots: More likely with stagnant air, wet foliage, or weak growth.

- Aphids/scale/mites: Can cause distorted growth, sticky honeydew, stippling, or general decline if severe.

- Borers: Small holes, sawdust-like frass, dieback, or bark damage can indicate a stressed tree being attacked.

## Danger Signs
<!-- Serious symptoms that may require urgent action or close inspection. Include signs of rot, severe dehydration, pest infestation, collapse, spreading black/brown tissue, or other plant-specific emergencies. -->

- Pot remains wet for many days and the tree declines.

- New growth wilts hard and does not recover after proper watering.

- Trunk base becomes soft, rotten-smelling, or unstable.

- Leaves rapidly brown across most of the canopy.

- Bark splits badly, oozes, or shows borer holes/frass.

- Roots smell rotten or appear black/mushy during inspection.

- No new growth after a long recovery period combined with worsening dieback.

## Rescue / Recovery
<!-- Guidance for stabilizing a stressed, neglected, newly purchased, damaged, or declining plant of this type. Focus on what to do first, what to avoid, and how quickly to intervene. -->

- Stabilize first: bright outdoor light, good airflow, and consistent but not soggy moisture.

- Do not fertilize until the tree is clearly growing again.

- Do not perform bonsai styling, heavy pruning, or root pruning while recovering.

- Check whether rain actually reaches the pot on a screened porch.

- If soil stays wet too long, improve drainage/airflow before adding more water.

- Remove only fully dead material unless disease/pest removal is necessary.

- Recovery from root damage can be slow; judge progress by buds, new shoots, and stable leaf condition.

## Hard No's
<!-- Things Dr. Veg should generally avoid recommending for this plant type because they are risky, harmful, or commonly misunderstood. -->

- Do not keep it indoors as a houseplant.

- Do not keep the pot sitting in standing water.

- Do not do aggressive root pruning or bonsai pot reduction while stressed.

- Do not fully defoliate the tree.

- Do not fertilize heavily after transplant shock or root damage.

- Do not bury the trunk flare or pile mulch against the trunk.

- Do not water purely by calendar.

- Do not treat harmless galls with harsh pesticides.

## Miscellaneous Notes
<!-- Useful care information that does not fit cleanly into the sections above. Keep this species/type-level, not specific to the user's individual plant. -->

- Southern live oak is a long-lived, broad-spreading native oak, not a small naturally compact tree.

- It can be trained as bonsai, but the safest path is slow development: recover first, build vigor, then gradually reduce roots and canopy over multiple seasons.

- Older leaves may yellow/drop before a spring flush; this alone is not automatically a crisis.

- In containers, water and root health matter much more than they would for an established landscape tree.
