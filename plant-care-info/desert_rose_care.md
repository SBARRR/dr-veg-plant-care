## Watering

- How to check: Check the top several inches of mix, pot weight, drainage speed, and caudex firmness. Water only when the mix has dried well; a slightly shriveled caudex can mean thirst, while a soft/mushy caudex can mean rot.
  <!-- How the user should judge whether watering is needed. Include practical methods like pot weight, finger check, tray level, leaf firmness, caudex feel, skewer test, or visual cues. -->

- Water type: Tap water is usually acceptable if not softened or extremely salty; rainwater is also fine.
  <!-- Type of water this plant can safely receive. Example: tap water acceptable, distilled/rain/RO only, avoid softened water, etc. -->

- Watering method: Top-water thoroughly until water drains out, then let the pot drain completely. Empty any tray or cachepot.

- Common watering mistakes: Watering on a calendar, keeping regular potting soil wet, watering during cool cloudy periods, misting instead of proper watering, or assuming leaf drop always means thirst.

## Maintenance

- Pests: Watch for spider mites, aphids, mealybugs, scale, and thrips. Check new growth, undersides of leaves, and stem joints. Treat only after identifying the pest; avoid oil sprays in hot direct sun.

## Seasonal Adjustments

- Spring / summer: Main growth and bloom season. Increase light, water deeply after dry-down, and fertilize lightly only if healthy and actively growing.
  <!-- Guidance for warm-season or active-growth care changes, such as watering frequency, light exposure, fertilizing, outdoor placement, heat management, or growth expectations. -->

- Fall / winter: Growth slows and leaf drop may happen. Reduce watering sharply, stop fertilizer, and protect from cold below about 50°F.

- Dormancy notes: Desert rose may become partly or fully leafless in cool/dry seasons. Leaf drop alone is not always a crisis; wet soil plus soft tissue is more concerning.

## Common Problems
<!-- Common non-emergency issues this plant type tends to have, including likely causes and early warning patterns. Use this for problems worth watching but not immediate crisis signs. -->

- Yellow leaves: Can come from overwatering, seasonal leaf drop, low light, pests, or root stress.

- Leaf drop: Often seasonal or stress-related; check temperature, soil moisture, and caudex firmness before reacting.

- No blooms: Usually too little light, immature plant, too much nitrogen, or not enough warm active growth.

- Leggy growth: Usually insufficient light.

- Wrinkled caudex: Often thirst if the mix is dry; concerning if paired with wet soil or mushy tissue.

- Pest pressure: Spider mites, aphids, mealybugs, scale, and thrips can distort growth or cause yellowing.

- Root rot: Most often caused by dense wet media, overwatering, cold wet conditions, or poor drainage.

## Danger Signs
<!-- Serious symptoms that may require urgent action or close inspection. Include signs of rot, severe dehydration, pest infestation, collapse, spreading black/brown tissue, or other plant-specific emergencies. -->

- Caudex, stem, or root crown feels soft, mushy, or collapses.

- Black/brown rot spreads along stems or from the base.

- Soil stays wet for many days after watering.

- Plant drops leaves while soil is wet and caudex is soft.

- Roots smell rotten or appear black/mushy.

- Severe pest infestation on new growth.

- Exposure to frost or prolonged cold below about 50°F.

## Rescue / Recovery
<!-- Guidance for stabilizing a stressed, neglected, newly purchased, damaged, or declining plant of this type. Focus on what to do first, what to avoid, and how quickly to intervene. -->

- Stabilize first: warm spot, bright light, excellent airflow, and no fertilizer.

- If soil is wet, do not water again until it dries and the plant is reassessed.

- If rot is suspected, check caudex firmness and consider inspecting roots.

- If the plant is in dense nursery soil, repot into fast-draining media once conditions are warm and the plant can handle disturbance.

- After repotting or root disturbance, wait several days to about a week before watering.

- If underwatered but otherwise firm/healthy, water deeply once and let drain fully.

- Treat pests only after confirming what pest is present.

## Hard No's
<!-- Things Dr. Veg should generally avoid recommending for this plant type because they are risky, harmful, or commonly misunderstood. -->

- Do not keep the soil constantly moist.

- Do not let it sit in a drip tray or cachepot of water.

- Do not use dense regular potting soil long-term.

- Do not water heavily during cool/dormant periods.

- Do not fertilize while stressed, cold, dormant, or recently repotted.

- Do not expose to frost or prolonged temperatures below about 50°F.

- Do not ignore a soft/mushy caudex.

- Do not spray neem/horticultural oil in hot direct sun.

- Do not assume it is pet-safe; sap/plant parts are toxic if eaten.

## Miscellaneous Notes
<!-- Useful care information that does not fit cleanly into the sections above. Keep this species/type-level, not specific to the user's individual plant. -->

- Desert rose is a caudiciform succulent, not a true rose.

- It is commonly grown as a bonsai-style plant because of its swollen caudex and sculptural branching.

- Strong light plus fast drainage are the two biggest success factors.

- Regular leaf drop can be normal during seasonal slowdown, shipping/store stress, or sudden care changes.

- The caudex is a major health clue: firm is good; mushy is bad; wrinkled with dry soil usually means thirsty.
